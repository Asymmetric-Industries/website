The Industrial
Reformation

Ten theses on Europe’s industrial future.
Asymmetric Industries · London, July 2026

Europe is in an undeclared state of industrial emergency. The companies that build what the continent cannot live without — the precision manufacturers, the power-component specialists, the engineering firms behind Airbus, Siemens, the grid, and NATO — are disappearing one retirement at a time. Nobody declared this emergency, so nobody is acting like it exists.

Europe’s problem is not its assets. The assets are the finest of their kind in the world. What is broken is the model that owns them and the tools they’ve been given. Both can be fixed.

These are our theses.

I

Europe is losing its industrial backbone one succession at a time.

Nearly half of German family firms have no successor. A hundred and ninety thousand European businesses went insolvent in a single year. This is not a downturn to be waited out; it is a structural unwinding of the capability layer beneath every European ambition — rearmament, grid modernisation, a space programme — each of which quietly assumes the suppliers will be there. They are leaving while the strategies are being written.

II

The mid-market is the scarcest asset in Europe.

The feel of a tolerance. The sound of a failing bearing. The judgment call on a weld that no manual captures. Certifications that took decades to earn; sole-source positions on thirty-year programmes; a workforce whose knowledge exists nowhere else on earth. These companies were built by people who bet the house and spent a lifetime honouring the bet. When one closes, nothing replaces it — you cannot recreate a certified special-process shop by hiring graduates. The world has never demanded more of these firms, and there have never been fewer of them.

III

AI has crossed a threshold. It has not crossed the factory door.

Something categorical has shifted: what took months now takes hours. And almost none of it has reached the people who run industrial businesses. The machinists, inspectors, planners, and plant managers still work from the same spreadsheets they had a decade ago. The technology is not the bottleneck. The way it is delivered — sold as seats, scoped as slideware, parked in pilot programmes — is.

IV

The operator is the alpha.

The expertise that lives in an operator — built over decades, irreplaceable when lost — is precisely the input AI needs to be useful. Pair them, and you build capacity that never existed. Separate them, and the AI degrades into a dashboard. Companies cutting their most experienced people to fund technology programmes are burning the fuel to buy the engine.

V

The person you cannot hire already works there.

Inside every industrial company is someone who has lived the operational pain, risen through the ranks, and carries a hundred ideas for how the business should run — blocked for years by budget cycles and org charts. A quality inspector who had never used more than Excel built his own computer-vision inspection system, alone, once someone handed him real tools and got out of the way. Every lasting transformation we have seen traces to finding this person. Every failure traces to never reaching them.

VI

Taste is the last scarce asset.

When AI labour is abundant, writing code is commoditised. What remains scarce is knowing exactly what to build and how the business should actually run. That knowledge lives at the top of companies and on the floor — rarely in between. The future belongs to whoever can find taste, protect it, and arm it.

VII

An enterprise is a thousand decisions a day.

Every decision is a loop: problem found, information gathered, lever pulled. Every loop is clogged — phone calls, reconciliation, meetings about who is right. Orders go to whichever supplier answers first; disruptions compound for days before anyone understands them. Scale used to be the moat. Decision speed is the new one — and it is the one advantage a thirty-million-euro company can win against a three-billion-euro one.

VIII

Every organisation has an immune system. It attacks the cure.

In large companies it is process: platform committees, gatekeepers, dashboards that contradict each other in the same board meeting. In the mid-market it is scar tissue: leaders burned by vendors who sold seats and vanished, by projects that detonated — and at a mid-market firm, one detonation is the company. So the pilot becomes the destination, the diagnosis comes from the top while the answer sits unasked on the floor, and the scepticism hardens. That scepticism is not backwardness. It is earned. Which is why trust here cannot be pitched — only demonstrated.

IX

Every other model leaks the alpha.

The consultant is blocked at the door and takes the knowledge when the engagement ends. The vendor sells the licence and skips the hard part. The fund watches the clock and sells at the wrong time. On one programme, a CFO said the quiet part aloud: “It is against our interests to go faster, because we will get paid less.” That sentence indicts a model, not a person. The value created inside these companies keeps escaping through the structures built to capture it. Only an owner cannot be blocked, cannot be scoped out, cannot be timed out.

X

A permanent home.

So we own. Not to strip, rename, and flip — to hold. The name stays on the gate. The people stay on the floor; they are the asset, and everything in these theses depends on them. There is no exit clock, because compounding rewards the patient and punishes the scheduled. What we bring is what these companies could never buy at fair terms: engineers who work where the work is, tools placed directly in the hands of the people who have been waiting for them, and an owner whose only way to win is for the company to become more itself — faster, prouder, harder to live without.
